% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Differential privacy (DP) is widely deployed in
federated learning to protect client data, and is
commonly assumed to reduce the effectiveness of
Byzantine attacks by masking malicious gradient
updates with additive Gaussian noise. We show that
this assumption is incorrect. We present
\textsc{DPAmplify}, a Byzantine attack that exploits
the analytical structure of the Gaussian DP
mechanism: by constructing adversarial gradients
with $\ell_2$-norm exactly equal to the clipping
threshold $C$, a Byzantine client ensures that
clipping is a no-op, and the zero-mean DP noise
becomes irrelevant in expectation. The expected
post-mechanism output is precisely aligned with any
chosen target direction, independent of the noise
level $\sigma$. We prove that $k$ Byzantine clients
using \textsc{DPAmplify} achieve a formal SNR
advantage characterized by two bounds: a closed-form
upper bound $kC/(\sigma\sqrt{n-k})$ and a tight bound
that accounts for honest gradient variance and matches
empirical results with 4.8\% relative error. The
attack operates in two phases: a passive estimation
phase in which Byzantine clients behave honestly
while estimating $C$ and $\sigma$ from observed
aggregate norms (undetectable by construction), and
an attack phase in which they submit adversarial
gradients with norms indistinguishable from honest
clients. We demonstrate empirically that
\textsc{DPAmplify} evades Krum and TrimmedMean, and
propose two targeted countermeasures: randomized
clipping threshold and gradient norm auditing.
\end{abstract}

% ── Introduction ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

Federated learning (FL) enables multiple clients to
collaboratively train a machine learning model without
sharing raw data~\cite{mcmahan2017communication}.
A critical concern in FL deployments is the presence
of Byzantine clients --- participants who submit
arbitrary, potentially malicious gradient updates.
Concurrently, differential privacy
(DP)~\cite{abadi2016deep} is widely deployed to
protect individual data contributions from inference
attacks. Together, Byzantine robustness and DP are
considered to provide ``defense in depth'' for
federated learning~\cite{yin2018byzantine}.

\textbf{The assumption we challenge.} The federated
learning security community implicitly treats DP
noise as an obstacle for Byzantine attackers: the
additive Gaussian noise masks the malicious signal,
reducing its effectiveness. This assumption is
reflected in the design of all known Byzantine
attacks~\cite{baruch2019little,
shejwalkar2021manipulating}, which are formulated
and evaluated in the absence of DP mechanisms.

\textbf{Our contribution.} We show that this
assumption is fundamentally incorrect. We present
\textsc{DPAmplify}, a Byzantine attack that treats
the Gaussian DP mechanism not as an obstacle but
as an \emph{analytical tool}. The key insight is
elementary: since the Gaussian noise in
$\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}(\mathbf{g})
= \mathrm{clip}(\mathbf{g}, C) + \boldsymbol{\xi}$
has zero mean, a Byzantine client can construct
$\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}$ such that
$\mathbb{E}[\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}
(\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}})]$ is precisely aligned
with any target direction, regardless of $\sigma$.
Setting $\|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}\|_2 = C$
exactly ensures that clipping is a no-op, and the
zero-mean noise becomes irrelevant in expectation.

We show that $k$ Byzantine clients using
\textsc{DPAmplify} achieve a formal SNR advantage
over honest participants, characterized by two
complementary bounds:
(i) a closed-form upper bound
$kC/(\sigma\sqrt{n-k})$, and
(ii) a tight bound that accounts for Byzantine
DP noise and honest gradient variance
(Theorems~\ref{thm:snr-upper}
and~\ref{thm:snr-tight}).

The tight bound reveals an important regime boundary:
when honest gradient variance $V_h$ is negligible
relative to DP noise (the IID setting), the upper
bound suffices; when $V_h \gtrsim \sigma^2/(n-k)$
(the non-IID or heterogeneous setting), the tight
bound is required for accurate predictions.

\textsc{DPAmplify} operates in two phases. In
Phase~1, Byzantine clients behave honestly while
passively estimating $C$ and $\sigma$ from observed
aggregate norms --- a process that is entirely
undetectable. In Phase~2, they switch to submitting
$\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}$, with gradient norms
indistinguishable from honest clients
(mean norm difference 0.019 in our experiments).
We demonstrate empirically that \textsc{DPAmplify}
evades Krum~\cite{blanchard2017machine} and
TrimmedMean~\cite{yin2018byzantine}.

\textbf{Relation to prior work.}
\textsc{DPAmplify} is conceptually complementary to
Robust-HDP~\cite{malekmohammadi2024robusthdp}, which
proposes a noise-aware \emph{aggregation} strategy
that estimates DP noise levels to improve utility.
While Robust-HDP exploits noise structure defensively,
\textsc{DPAmplify} exploits the same structure
offensively. Together, these results demonstrate that
knowledge of the DP mechanism's analytical structure
is powerful on both sides of the security boundary.

\textbf{Scope.} This attack applies to FL deployments
using the Gaussian DP mechanism without Secure
Aggregation (SecAgg). Settings with SecAgg,
trusted execution environments, or cryptographic
gradient verification are explicitly out of scope
(Section~\ref{sec:threat}).

\textbf{Contributions.} We make the following
contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We identify a previously uncharacterized
    attack vector in DP-FL: the analytical structure
    of the Gaussian mechanism can be exploited
    offensively by Byzantine clients.
    \item We present \textsc{DPAmplify}, a two-phase
    attack with a passive undetectable estimation
    phase and an optimized adversarial phase.
    \item We prove two complementary SNR bounds
    (Theorems~\ref{thm:snr-upper}
    and~\ref{thm:snr-tight}) and
    identify the regime boundary between them.
    \item We validate the tight bound empirically
    with relative error 4.8\%, and demonstrate
    evasion of Krum and TrimmedMean.
    \item We propose two targeted countermeasures:
    randomized clipping and gradient norm auditing.
\end{itemize}

% ── Threat Model ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Threat Model}
\label{sec:threat}

We consider a standard cross-silo federated learning system
comprising $n$ clients and a central server. The server
aggregates client updates using a differentially-private
variant of FedAvg~\cite{mcmahan2017communication}: each
client clips its local gradient to $\ell_2$-norm $C$ and
adds Gaussian noise $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2
\mathbf{I})$ before transmission, following the DP-SGD
framework of Abadi et al.~\cite{abadi2016deep}.

\textbf{Attacker.} We assume $k < n/2$ Byzantine clients
under the control of a single adversary. The remaining
$n - k$ clients are honest and follow the protocol
faithfully.

\textbf{Attacker capabilities.} The adversary
(i) controls the gradient updates submitted by its $k$
clients, and
(ii) observes the aggregated model parameters broadcast
by the server after each round.
The adversary cannot modify the server-side aggregation
rule, access other clients' local data, or alter other
clients' updates. We make no assumption about whether the
server is trusted; all our attack analysis holds even
under an honest-but-curious server.

\textbf{Attacker knowledge.} The adversary has black-box
access to the federated system. It does not know $C$ or
$\sigma$ a priori, but can estimate them passively from
the sequence of observed aggregate norms across rounds,
as described in Section~\ref{sec:attack-phase1}. This
estimation phase requires no anomalous behavior from the
Byzantine clients and is therefore undetectable.

\textbf{Adversarial goal.} The adversary wishes to steer
the global model toward a target direction
$\mathbf{g}_{\text{target}} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ of its
choosing. This encompasses targeted backdoor attacks
(where $\mathbf{g}_{\text{target}}$ is the gradient of
a loss designed to misclassify a specific input class)
and untargeted model degradation attacks.

\textbf{Out of scope.} We do not consider settings that
employ Secure Aggregation (SecAgg), trusted execution
environments, or cryptographic gradient verification
protocols. These mechanisms prevent the server from
observing individual client updates and therefore
preclude the class of attacks we study. We consider
this an important direction for future work.

% ── Background ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Background}

\subsection{Federated Learning with Differential Privacy}

In federated learning, $n$ clients collaboratively train a
global model $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ without sharing raw data.
At each round $t$, the server broadcasts the current global
model to all clients. Each client $i$ computes a local
gradient $\mathbf{g}_i^t$ on its private dataset and
transmits a privatized update to the server, which
aggregates them via FedAvg:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\theta}^{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}^t
    - \frac{\eta}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n}
    \mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}(\mathbf{g}_i^t)
\end{equation}
where $\eta$ is the global learning rate and
$\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}$ is the Gaussian DP mechanism.

\textbf{Gaussian DP mechanism.} Following
Abadi et al.~\cite{abadi2016deep}, the mechanism is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}(\mathbf{g})
    = \mathrm{clip}(\mathbf{g}, C) + \boldsymbol{\xi},
    \quad
    \boldsymbol{\xi} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},
    \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}_d)
    \label{eq:dp-mechanism}
\end{equation}
where the $\ell_2$-clipping operation is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{clip}(\mathbf{g}, C)
    = \mathbf{g} \cdot
    \min\!\left(1,\, \frac{C}{\|\mathbf{g}\|_2}\right)
    \label{eq:clipping}
\end{equation}
Clipping bounds the sensitivity of each update to $C$,
and the additive Gaussian noise with standard deviation
$\sigma$ provides $(\varepsilon, \delta)$-differential
privacy for an appropriate choice of $\sigma$ relative
to $\varepsilon$ and $\delta$~\cite{abadi2016deep}.

A key property of this mechanism, which forms the
foundation of our attack, is that the noise has zero mean:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\bigl[\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}(\mathbf{g})
    \bigr]
    = \mathrm{clip}(\mathbf{g}, C)
    \label{eq:zero-mean}
\end{equation}
This follows immediately from $\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\xi}]
= \mathbf{0}$.

\subsection{Byzantine Attacks in Federated Learning}

A Byzantine client can submit an arbitrary update
$\tilde{\mathbf{g}}_i^t$ to the server regardless of
its local data. Prior work has studied this threat
extensively~\cite{blanchard2017machine,yin2018byzantine,
baruch2019little,shejwalkar2021manipulating}, and several
Byzantine-robust aggregation rules have been proposed
as defenses, including Krum~\cite{blanchard2017machine}
and coordinate-wise TrimmedMean~\cite{yin2018byzantine}.

\textbf{The implicit assumption in the literature.}
All existing Byzantine attack
methods~\cite{baruch2019little,shejwalkar2021manipulating}
are designed and analyzed without accounting for the
presence of DP noise. Implicitly, DP is treated as an
additional obstacle that the attacker must overcome:
the noise masks the malicious signal, reducing attack
effectiveness. To our knowledge, no prior work has
asked the converse question --- can the structure of the
DP mechanism be exploited \emph{offensively} to amplify
a Byzantine attack?

\textbf{Our position.} We show that this assumption is
incorrect. The linearity of expectation through the
Gaussian mechanism (Equation~\ref{eq:zero-mean}) enables
a Byzantine client to construct gradients whose
\emph{expected} post-mechanism output is precisely
aligned with any target direction, independent of the
noise level $\sigma$. The noise does not mask the
adversarial signal --- it merely adds zero-mean variance
that averages out over rounds.

% ── The DPAmplify Attack ──────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{The DPAmplify Attack}
\label{sec:attack}

DPAmplify proceeds in two phases. In Phase~1
(Section~\ref{sec:attack-phase1}), Byzantine clients
behave honestly while passively estimating the DP
parameters $C$ and $\sigma$. In Phase~2
(Section~\ref{sec:attack-phase2}), they submit
adversarial gradients optimized to exploit the structure
of $\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}$.

\subsection{Phase 1: Passive Parameter Estimation}
\label{sec:attack-phase1}

The adversary needs estimates of the clipping threshold
$C$ and noise standard deviation $\sigma$ to construct
optimal adversarial gradients. We show these can be
obtained without any anomalous behavior.

During rounds $t = 1, \ldots, T_{\mathrm{est}}$, each
Byzantine client submits honest gradient updates
computed from its local data. After each round, the
adversary observes the $\ell_2$-norm of the aggregated
update broadcast by the server:
\begin{equation}
    \rho^t = \left\|\boldsymbol{\theta}^t
    - \boldsymbol{\theta}^{t-1}\right\|_2
\end{equation}
Since each honest gradient is clipped to norm at most
$C$ before aggregation, the distribution of $\rho^t$
concentrates near $C$ for large $n$. The adversary
estimates:
\begin{align}
    \hat{C}
    &= \mathrm{percentile}_{90}
       \bigl(\{\rho^1, \ldots, \rho^{T_{\mathrm{est}}}
       \}\bigr)
    \label{eq:C-estimate} \\
    \hat{\sigma}
    &= \mathrm{std}
       \bigl(\{\rho^1, \ldots, \rho^{T_{\mathrm{est}}}
       \}\bigr)
    \label{eq:sigma-estimate}
\end{align}
\textbf{Undetectability.} During Phase~1, Byzantine clients
are indistinguishable from honest clients: they submit
real gradients computed on real data, and their update
norms follow the same distribution as honest clients.
No norm-based or statistics-based anomaly detector can
flag them during this phase.

\subsection{Phase 2: Adversarial Gradient Construction}
\label{sec:attack-phase2}

The core observation enabling DPAmplify is the following.

\begin{lemma}[Clipping-free adversarial gradient]
\label{lem:no-clip}
Let $\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}
= C \cdot (\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}} /
\|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}\|_2)$.
Then $\|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}\|_2 = C$ and
$\mathrm{clip}(\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}, C)
= \mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
$\|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}\|_2
= C \cdot \|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}
/ \|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}\|_2\|_2 = C$.
Since $\|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}\|_2 = C \leq C$,
$\min(1, C/\|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}\|_2) = 1$,
so $\mathrm{clip}(\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}, C)
= \mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Zero-bias adversarial output]
\label{cor:zero-bias}
$\mathbb{E}\bigl[\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}
(\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}})\bigr]
= \mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:no-clip} and
Equation~\ref{eq:zero-mean}.
\end{proof}

Starting from round $T_{\mathrm{est}} + 1$, each of the
$k$ Byzantine clients submits:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\mathbf{g}}_i^t
    = \hat{C} \cdot
    \frac{\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}}
    {\|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}\|_2}
    \quad \forall\, i \in \mathcal{B},\;
    t > T_{\mathrm{est}}
    \label{eq:g-adv}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{B}$ is the set of Byzantine clients.
By Corollary~\ref{cor:zero-bias}, the expected
contribution of each Byzantine client to the FedAvg
aggregate is exactly $\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}$,
aligned with $\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}$.

The complete DPAmplify procedure is summarized in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:dpamplify}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{DPAmplify Byzantine Client}
\label{alg:dpamplify}
\SetAlgoLined
\KwIn{Target direction $\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}$,
      estimation window $T_{\mathrm{est}}$,
      local dataset $\mathcal{D}_i$}
\KwOut{Gradient updates for each round $t$}
Initialize estimator $\mathcal{E}$\;
\For{$t = 1, 2, \ldots$}{
    Receive global model $\boldsymbol{\theta}^t$ from server\;
    \eIf{$t \leq T_{\mathrm{est}}$}{
        $\mathbf{g}_i^t \leftarrow
        \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
        \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}^t;\,
        \mathcal{D}_i)$
        \tcp*{honest gradient}
        $\mathcal{E}.\mathrm{update}
        (\|\mathbf{g}_i^t\|_2)$\;
        \textbf{submit} $\mathbf{g}_i^t$\;
    }{
        $\hat{C} \leftarrow \mathcal{E}
        .\mathrm{estimate\_C}()$\;
        $\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}} \leftarrow
        \hat{C} \cdot \mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}
        / \|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}\|_2$\;
        \textbf{submit} $\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}$\;
    }
}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis}
\label{sec:theory}

We now characterize the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
advantage of DPAmplify over honest gradient contributions.

\begin{theorem}[SNR Upper Bound]
\label{thm:snr-upper}
Under DPAmplify with $k$ Byzantine clients and FedAvg
aggregation, the Byzantine signal projected onto
$\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}$ satisfies:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{SNR}_{\mathrm{attack}}
    \leq \frac{k \cdot C}{\sigma \sqrt{n - k}}
    \label{eq:snr-upper}
\end{equation}
This bound is tight when honest gradient variance
projected onto $\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}$ is
negligible relative to DP noise.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[SNR Tight Bound]
\label{thm:snr-tight}
Let $V_h$ denote the per-client variance of honest
gradients projected onto $\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{target}}$:
$V_h = \mathrm{Var}\bigl[\mathrm{clip}(\mathbf{g}_h, C)
\cdot \hat{\mathbf{g}}_{\mathrm{target}}\bigr]$,
where $\hat{\mathbf{g}}_{\mathrm{target}}$ is the unit
vector. Then:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{SNR}_{\mathrm{attack}}
    = \frac{k C / n}
    {\sqrt{\sigma^2/n
    + (n-k)\, V_h / n^2}}
    \label{eq:snr-tight}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The FedAvg aggregate projected onto
$\hat{\mathbf{g}}_{\mathrm{target}}$ decomposes as:
\begin{equation*}
    P^t = \underbrace{
    \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}}
    \mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}(\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}})
    \cdot \hat{\mathbf{g}}_{\mathrm{target}}
    }_{\text{Byzantine signal}}
    +
    \underbrace{
    \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i \notin \mathcal{B}}
    \mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}(\mathbf{g}_i^t)
    \cdot \hat{\mathbf{g}}_{\mathrm{target}}
    }_{\text{honest noise}}
\end{equation*}
\textit{Byzantine signal:} By Corollary~\ref{cor:zero-bias},
$\mathbb{E}[\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}
(\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}})
\cdot \hat{\mathbf{g}}_{\mathrm{target}}]
= C$. Summing over $k$ clients and dividing by $n$:
$\mu_{\mathrm{byz}} = kC/n$.

\textit{Variance:} The total variance of $P^t$ is:
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{Var}[P^t]
    &= \frac{k \sigma^2}{n^2}
    + \frac{(n-k)\sigma^2}{n^2}
    + \frac{(n-k) V_h}{n^2} \\
    &= \frac{\sigma^2}{n}
    + \frac{(n-k) V_h}{n^2}
\end{align*}
The first two terms arise from DP noise on Byzantine
and honest clients respectively; the third from honest
gradient variance. Taking the ratio:
$\mathrm{SNR} = \mu_{\mathrm{byz}}
/ \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[P^t]}$,
which gives Equation~\ref{eq:snr-tight}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Regime boundary]
Theorem~\ref{thm:snr-upper} is a tight approximation
of Theorem~\ref{thm:snr-tight} when $V_h \ll \sigma^2/(n-k)$,
i.e., when DP noise dominates honest gradient variance.
When $V_h \gtrsim \sigma^2/(n-k)$ --- the non-IID or
heterogeneous data regime --- the tight bound
(Equation~\ref{eq:snr-tight}) is required for accurate
predictions. In both regimes, $\mathrm{SNR}_{\mathrm{attack}}
> 1$ is achievable with $k \geq \lceil \sigma\sqrt{n}/C
\rceil$ Byzantine clients, which is a small fraction
of $n$ for typical DP parameter choices.
\end{remark}

\textbf{Empirical validation.} We verify these bounds
experimentally in Section~\ref{sec:eval-snr}:
Theorem~\ref{thm:snr-upper} overestimates the empirical
SNR by 35.6\% (as expected for a bound), while
Theorem~\ref{thm:snr-tight} matches the empirical SNR
with a relative error of 4.8\%.

% ── Related Work ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Byzantine attacks on federated learning.}
Blanchard et al.~\cite{blanchard2017machine} introduced
the Byzantine fault model for distributed gradient
descent and proposed Krum as a robust aggregator.
Subsequent work by Yin et al.~\cite{yin2018byzantine}
established optimal statistical rates for TrimmedMean
and coordinate-wise median under Byzantine faults.
Baruch et al.~\cite{baruch2019little} showed that
small, statistically inconspicuous perturbations
(``a little is enough'') can circumvent robust
aggregators. Shejwalkar and Houmansadr~\cite{
shejwalkar2021manipulating} proposed MinMax and MinSum,
which optimize the adversarial perturbation subject to
evasion constraints. Cao et al.~\cite{cao2022fltrust}
proposed FLTrust, which uses a server-side root dataset
to assign trust scores to client updates.

\textbf{Key distinction.} All of the above attacks are
designed and analyzed without any differential privacy
mechanism. They treat the DP noise, when present, as
a confounding factor that reduces attack effectiveness.
DPAmplify is the first attack that treats the structure
of the Gaussian DP mechanism as an \emph{offensive
tool}: by setting $\|\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}\|_2 = C$
exactly, the adversary ensures that clipping is a
no-op and $\mathbb{E}[\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{DP}}
(\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}})] = \mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}$,
exploiting the zero-mean property of Gaussian noise
rather than fighting it.

\textbf{Noise-aware federated learning (defensive).}
Malekmohammadi et al.~\cite{malekmohammadi2024robusthdp}
propose a noise-aware \emph{aggregation}
algorithm that estimates the true DP noise level in
each client's update and assigns aggregation weights
inversely proportional to estimated noise variance.
This improves utility in heterogeneous DP settings.
DPAmplify is conceptually orthogonal: it is a
noise-aware \emph{attack} that exploits the same
analytical structure from the adversary's perspective.
The two works together demonstrate that knowledge of
the DP noise structure is powerful on both sides of
the security boundary.

\textbf{Privacy attacks on federated learning.}
Gradient inversion attacks~\cite{abadi2016deep} aim
to reconstruct training data from gradient updates.
These are privacy attacks, distinct from the
Byzantine poisoning attacks studied here. DPAmplify
does not attempt to recover private data; it attempts
to steer the global model.

\textbf{Robust aggregation under DP.}
Recent work has studied the joint problem of robustness
and privacy in federated learning~\cite{yin2018byzantine}.
These works generally assume that DP noise hurts the
attacker and focus on designing aggregators that remain
robust despite the additional noise. Our work shows
that this assumption is flawed: a sufficiently
informed attacker can turn the DP noise from a
hindrance into an asset.

% ── Countermeasures ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Countermeasures}
\label{sec:countermeasures}

We propose two defenses targeting the specific
structural properties that DPAmplify exploits.

\subsection{Randomized Clipping Threshold}

DPAmplify's Phase~1 relies on a stable clipping
threshold $C$ to produce a reliable estimate $\hat{C}$.
If $C$ is randomized each round by sampling
$C_t \sim \mathcal{U}(C_{\min}, C_{\max})$, the
passive estimator observes a mixture distribution and
cannot converge to a stable value.

\textbf{Effect on the attack.} With randomized
clipping, the adversarial gradient from
Equation~\ref{eq:g-adv} becomes
$\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}^t =
\hat{C} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{g}}_{\mathrm{target}}$,
where $\hat{C}$ is an inaccurate estimate. The
adversarial contribution per round is no longer
$C_t \cdot \hat{\mathbf{g}}_{\mathrm{target}}$ but
$\hat{C} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{g}}_{\mathrm{target}}$,
introducing a systematic error of magnitude
$|C_t - \hat{C}|$ that does not average out.
When $C_{\max} - C_{\min}$ is large relative to $C$,
this degrades Phase~2 substantially.

\textbf{Overhead.} Randomized clipping changes only
the choice of $C_t$ each round and adds no
computational cost. The privacy accounting must be
updated to use $C_{\max}$ as the sensitivity bound.

\subsection{Gradient Norm Auditing}

DPAmplify always submits gradients with
$\|\tilde{\mathbf{g}}_i\|_2 = C$ \emph{exactly}.
In contrast, honest gradients have norms that vary
stochastically around $C$ due to their data-dependent
direction. This structural signature is detectable.

\textbf{Detection rule.} For each client $i$, the
server maintains a running history of submitted
gradient norms $\{\|\tilde{\mathbf{g}}_i^t\|_2\}$.
A client is flagged if:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\mathrm{std}(\{\|\tilde{\mathbf{g}}_i^t
    \|_2\}_{t \in W})}
    {\mathrm{mean}(\{\|\tilde{\mathbf{g}}_i^t
    \|_2\}_{t \in W})}
    < \tau
    \label{eq:audit}
\end{equation}
where $W$ is a sliding window of recent rounds and
$\tau$ is a detection threshold. An honest client
whose gradient norms vary naturally will have a
coefficient of variation well above $\tau$; a
DPAmplify client whose norms are locked to $\hat{C}$
will have a near-zero coefficient of variation.

\textbf{Limitation.} An adaptive attacker who adds
artificial norm jitter to $\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}$
can evade this detector. Specifically, multiplying
$\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{adv}}$ by a random scalar
$s \sim \mathcal{U}(0.9, 1.0)$ before submission
introduces norm variation while only marginally
reducing the adversarial signal. We leave the design
of detectors robust to such adaptive adversaries as
future work.

% ── Evaluation ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

All experiments use the configuration validated in our
proof of concept: $n = 20$ clients, $k = 3$ Byzantine
clients (15\%), clipping threshold $C = 1.0$, noise
multiplier $\sigma = 0.1$, and a 2-layer MLP
(784$\to$128$\to$10) trained on MNIST. The passive
estimation phase uses $T_{\mathrm{est}} = 20$ rounds,
followed by $T_{\mathrm{attack}} = 100$ attack rounds.
We evaluate three aggregation rules: FedAvg, Krum
with $f=3$, and TrimmedMean with $\beta = 0.1$.
All experiments are run with fixed random seed 42
for reproducibility.

\subsection{SNR Validation}
\label{sec:eval-snr}

Figure~\ref{fig:snr-validation} compares the
empirical SNR --- measured as the ratio of the mean to
the standard deviation of the Byzantine signal
projected onto $\hat{\mathbf{g}}_{\mathrm{target}}$
over 300 simulated rounds --- against the two theoretical
bounds across a range of $\sigma$ and $k$ values.

The upper bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:snr-upper})
consistently overestimates the empirical SNR,
with relative errors ranging from 20\% to 40\%.
The tight bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:snr-tight})
tracks the empirical SNR closely across all
configurations, with a maximum relative error
of 4.8\% at the validated baseline configuration.
This confirms that accounting for honest gradient
variance $V_h$ is essential for accurate predictions
in heterogeneous settings.

\section{Conclusion}

DPAmplify demonstrates that the Gaussian DP mechanism can be exploited
by a Byzantine client to coherently steer the global model update.  We
provide (i) a zero-bias construction, (ii) an evasion argument based
on norm indistinguishability, and (iii) both an upper-bound and a tight
SNR formula that together characterise when the attack succeeds and
when honest gradient heterogeneity naturally limits its effectiveness.
